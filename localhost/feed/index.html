In a way, the state Legislature is in a bit of a unique position to address issues that have been in the state’s consciousness for decades. Unlike a litmus test election, where party allegiance and ideological tilt are central to the campaign, Virginia’s peculiar voting system presents lawmakers with a choice. Do they continue to follow the path of Thomas Jefferson, who guided them with the doctrine of representative government, or do they follow the path laid out by Virginia’s authors, who were abolitionists, or its present Supreme Court, which was at the forefront of the push for gay marriage?
Most would argue the answer is not to follow Jefferson or to follow the courts. So much of Virginia’s contemporary political culture is grounded in Jefferson and his vision of the ideal legislator, a people’s representative. The constitutional powers of the Virginia Legislature are limited to enacting laws based on those delegated to the federal government. This state repubic’s neglect of this distinction during the Civil War led to the authorization of the federal government to take a portion of Virginia’s land, a process that would define Virginia’s political relationships with Washington for the next two centuries. This bit of known knowledge informs nearly every statute, state administrative law, or judicial rule the Virginia Legislature considers.
This history has not, however, prevented Virginia from slipping backward.
Black voter turnout has been in decline. The state has twice engaged in the admissions practices that have been used to deny African-Americans access to college. As a result, Virginia’s African-American voter turnout has declined, among other reasons, because it is harder to register, it is harder to vote early and it is harder to vote absentee on Election Day.
As a result, despite being the nation’s 13th largest state, Virginia has fewer African-American elected officials than any other state in the country.

Here’s how Virginia defines a “lawful” gun owner:
A person who owns, possesses, or has access to a legally permitted firearm, firearm accessory, or ammunition in Virginia, is a lawful owner of that firearm or firearm accessory. A person is a lawful owner of that firearm or firearm accessory if he, her, or they, as on or after December 31, 2015, are legally authorized by law to possess, possess, or use that firearm or firearm accessory. What’s the specific law?
The measure was meant to expand the ability of gun owners to possess firearms in some instances in the state without the need for a license.
